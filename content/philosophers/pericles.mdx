## A brief orientation

Pericles was an Athenian aristocrat (his mother's family, the
Alcmaeonidae, were prominent for generations before him) whose
career carried him from his first political action as a young man
through three decades of nearly continuous influence over Athenian
politics. He held the strategia — the elected generalship — fifteen
times. Under his political direction Athens rebuilt the temples of
the Acropolis after the Persian destruction, consolidated the
Delian League into the Athenian *arche* (empire), and entered the
long Peloponnesian War against Sparta. He died of the plague in 429
BCE in the second year of the war.

## The sources

The principal contemporary source is Thucydides, who knew him,
served as an Athenian general in the war Pericles started, and gave
us — in the Funeral Oration of <cite>History</cite> Book II — the
most influential single ancient statement of the Athenian
self-conception under his leadership. Plutarch's <cite>Life</cite>
is the long biographical treatment. Plato's <cite>Gorgias</cite>
and <cite>Menexenus</cite> engage with him critically.

## What the Periclean Athens was

The Athens Pericles led was a democracy with an empire — an
unusual combination of forms. The Athenian *demos* met every nine
days in assembly and decided foreign and domestic policy by direct
vote; the empire that produced the revenue paid the rowers of the
fleet and built the temples. Thucydides' famous summary judgement
on Pericles — that the city was nominally a democracy but in fact
ruled by its first citizen — is the locus classicus of the question
of how a republic can be effectively led without being thereby
unmade as a republic.

## Why he matters for Virtue & Power

Pericles is the platform's case for the statesman who leads a
democracy through extended sustained policy without seizing it. The
contrast with the late Roman Republic is direct: Pericles' Athens
did not need a Caesar because Pericles held office under the
ordinary institutions and kept them. That this was harder to sustain
after his death is part of the long Greek argument the platform's
later entries take up.
