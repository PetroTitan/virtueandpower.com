## A note on historicity

Whether a single historical Lycurgus founded the Spartan constitution
or whether the tradition gathered an accreted body of customs around
a half-legendary lawgiver is a question modern scholarship has been
arguing for two centuries. The platform treats it as open. Plutarch
himself, who gives us the longest ancient <cite>Life</cite> of
Lycurgus, opens by acknowledging that scarcely anything reported of
him is undisputed; he tells the story anyway because the story is
load-bearing. We do the same.

## What the Spartan tradition credits him with

The Spartan constitution attributed to Lycurgus combines several
elements unusual among Greek polities of the period. A dual kingship
(two kings drawn from two royal houses) sits alongside a council of
elders (the *gerousia*), an assembly of citizens, and the five annually
elected ephors. The *agōgē* — the public upbringing that begins at
the age of seven and forms a Spartan boy into a soldier-citizen —
is the institutional spine of the system. The *syssitia* (common
meals) and the prohibition of conspicuous wealth complete it.

## Why the classical tradition kept reading him

The Greek philosophical tradition could not stop arguing about
Sparta. Plato's <cite>Republic</cite>, the timocratic regime of Books
VIII–IX, is a transparent meditation on the Spartan form. Aristotle's
<cite>Politics</cite> dedicates serious analysis to the Lacedaemonian
constitution. Xenophon, who admired Sparta directly, wrote the
<cite>Lacedaemonian Constitution</cite> in defence of it. Plutarch's
<cite>Life</cite> is the form in which the early-modern republican
tradition received the figure — Rousseau, Montesquieu and the
American founders all read Plutarch's Lycurgus seriously.
