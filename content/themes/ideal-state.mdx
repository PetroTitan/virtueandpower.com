## The city in speech

The ideal state is the perfectly just city Plato constructs "in speech" in
the [Republic](/books/republic). The platform reads it as the most famous
and most misread political construction in the history of thought. Plato
builds it not, in the first instance, as a practical blueprint but as a
*model* — justice "writ large," easier to discern in the city than in the
individual soul — and the platform reads much of the misreading of the
Republic as the failure to grasp that the ideal state is primarily a
device for understanding [justice](/themes/justice-in-plato), and only
secondarily, if at all, a programme for reform.

## The structure

The platform reads the ideal state as a city of three classes mirroring the
three parts of the soul: the producers (appetite), the guardian-soldiers
(spirit), and the philosopher-rulers (reason). Its most radical and
notorious features belong to the guardian class: the abolition of private
property and private family among the rulers, the communal rearing of
children, the equal participation of women in the highest offices, and the
rigorous selection and education that produces the [philosopher-king](/themes/philosopher-king).
The platform reads these proposals as following from a single principle —
that the rulers must have no private interest that could compete with the
good of the whole — rather than as arbitrary radicalism.

## Blueprint or measure?

The platform reads the central interpretive question as whether Plato meant
the ideal state to be *built*. He says it exists "perhaps as a pattern laid
up in heaven," that whether it could ever be realised is a separate question,
and that the philosopher who has seen it will order his own soul by it
whether or not the city exists. The platform reads the ideal state primarily
as a *measure* — a standard of justice against which every actual
constitution falls short — and reads Plato's later turn in the
[Laws](/books/the-laws) toward a detailed, workable second-best city as his
own acknowledgement that the ideal is not a practical programme.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

The ideal state is the platform's central case of utopian political
thought — the construction of the perfectly just city as a way of thinking
about justice itself — and the foundation of a tradition that runs from More
and the Renaissance utopias to the modern critics who read it as the first
totalitarian blueprint. The platform reads it without either reverence or
the easy modern dismissal, in [the Republic
misunderstood](/essays/the-republic-misunderstood).
