## Friendship as a political bond

Aristotle devotes two of the ten books of the [Nicomachean
Ethics](/books/nicomachean-ethics) to friendship (*philia*) — more than to any
other single topic — and the platform reads this as a sign of how central he
takes it to be, not only to the good life of the individual but to the life of
the city. His striking claim is that friendship is a *political* force:
"friendship seems to hold cities together, and lawgivers seem to care more about
it than about justice." The platform reads this as one of his most distinctive
and underappreciated political insights — that the bonds of affection and shared
life, not only the structures of law and justice, are what make a community
cohere.

## The kinds of friendship

The platform reads Aristotle's analysis of the three kinds of friendship as the
foundation. Friendships of *utility* (based on mutual advantage) and of
*pleasure* (based on enjoyment) are real but unstable, lasting only as long as
the benefit or the pleasure; the highest friendship, of *virtue* (or character),
is the bond between good people who wish each other well for their own sake, and
it is rare, lasting and the truest form. The platform reads the lower forms as
nonetheless politically important: a city is held together by countless
friendships of utility and pleasure — the working relationships, shared
enjoyments and reciprocal goodwill of fellow citizens — that bind people who
will never be virtue-friends into a common life.

## Civic friendship

The platform reads Aristotle's concept of *civic friendship* (*homonoia*,
"concord" or "like-mindedness") as his deepest political application of the
theme. A well-functioning city is bound not only by justice and law but by a
kind of friendship among its citizens — a shared commitment to the common good,
a willingness to cooperate, a mutual goodwill that makes them treat one another
as partners rather than rivals. The platform reads this under [citizenship in
Aristotle](/themes/citizenship-in-aristotle): the reciprocity of ruling and
being ruled rests on civic friendship, and its breakdown into faction is the
death of the city. Where there is genuine concord, Aristotle holds, the city
hardly needs justice at all.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

Friendship and politics is one of Aristotle's most original contributions to
political thought — the recognition that the affective bonds of community are as
load-bearing as its institutions, a truth that purely structural and legal
accounts of politics tend to miss. The platform reads it alongside Xenophon's
[friendship and loyalty](/themes/friendship-and-loyalty) and develops it in
[friendship as political force](/essays/friendship-as-political-force).
