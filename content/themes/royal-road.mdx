## The empire's nervous system

The Royal Road is the single most legible piece of Achaemenid
statecraft. Built and organised under Darius I, the great trunk road
ran roughly 2,700 kilometres from Sardis near the Aegean coast to Susa
in the Persian heartland, with further routes branching toward the
other capitals and the eastern provinces. The platform reads it as the
empire's nervous system — the physical infrastructure that turned a
scattering of conquered territories into a single governable body.

## Speed as power

What made the road decisive was not the pavement but the *relay
system* that ran along it. Herodotus describes the imperial courier
service (the *angareion*): fresh riders and horses stationed at regular
intervals, so that a message could be passed from hand to hand across
the whole distance far faster than any single traveller could manage —
Herodotus reckons in days what an ordinary journey took in months. His
description of the couriers, whom neither snow nor rain nor heat nor
darkness stays from their appointed stages, became the most famous
ancient tribute to a postal service. The platform reads the road under
[imperial communication](/themes/imperial-communication): the king who
could learn of a frontier revolt in a week and answer it in another
held his empire on a far tighter rein than one who learned of it in a
season.

## Roads, logistics, and control

The road did triple duty. It carried the king's messages and his
inspectors; it carried his armies and their supplies, so that force
could be projected to a troubled province quickly; and it carried the
trade and tribute that funded the state. The platform reads it
alongside [imperial logistics](/themes/imperial-logistics) and
[satrapies](/themes/satrapies) as one of the three load-bearing
systems of Persian governance — the others being the provincial
administration and the tribute economy. Together they made
[governance at scale](/themes/governance-at-scale) possible.

## The long afterlife

The Persian road system was inherited by Alexander and the Hellenistic
kingdoms, and its principle — that an empire is held together by its
roads and its posts — runs directly to the Roman *cursus publicus* and
the road network the platform reads on the Roman hubs. The Royal Road
is the ancient world's founding demonstration that the reach of a
state is set by the speed of its communications. Every later empire
that built roads and relays to govern distance was, knowingly or not,
working in the tradition Darius established.
