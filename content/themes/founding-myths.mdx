## The story a polity tells about its own beginning

A founding myth is the narrative by which a community accounts for its
own origin — and, in accounting for it, makes its present arrangements
feel necessary rather than accidental. Rome had Romulus and the
she-wolf, the fratricide, and the augury that settled the city's site;
it had Numa receiving the religious law from the nymph Egeria. Sparta
had Lycurgus and the Delphic oracle that sanctioned his constitution.
China had the sage-kings of high antiquity and the Mandate of Heaven.
The platform reads these not as failed history but as a distinct kind
of political work — the work of making a beginning *legible* and
*authoritative* after the fact.

## Why the myth is load-bearing

The founding myth does something a sober chronicle cannot. A founding,
by its nature, lacks prior authorisation — there is no constitution
yet to make the founder legitimate, no settled law to make his law
binding. The myth supplies, retrospectively, the legitimacy the
founding could not supply for itself: it places the origin under
divine sanction, or in continuity with a venerated past, or at the
hands of a figure too extraordinary to be judged by ordinary measures.
This is why the platform reads [political legitimacy](/themes/political-legitimacy)
and founding myth as two sides of one structure. Plutarch, who knew
perfectly well that the stories of Lycurgus and Numa were uncertain,
told them anyway, "because the story is load-bearing."

## Reading the myth without credulity or contempt

The platform's discipline here is exact: it neither takes founding
myths as factual history nor dismisses them as mere propaganda. The
historicity of Lycurgus is genuinely open; the she-wolf is plainly
legend; the Mandate of Heaven is a theological-political doctrine, not
an event. But the *function* of these stories is historical and real —
they shaped how Romans, Spartans and Chinese understood what their
polities were for and what bound them together. To read a founding
myth well is to read it for what it did, not only for whether it
happened.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

Founding myths are where this cluster meets the platform's older theme
of [historical memory](/themes/historical-memory). A polity that
remembers its founding in a particular way is, in part, governed by
that memory. The platform reads the founding myth as one of the oldest
instruments of [institution-building](/themes/institution-building):
before there are durable offices, there is a durable story, and the
story is often what the offices are built to honour.
