## The classical inquiry

A republic — Latin *res publica*, the public thing — is the political
form in which authority is shared rather than concentrated, in which
magistracies are limited in duration and answerable to others, and in
which legitimacy is taken to derive from the citizen body rather than
from a single person or family. The classical tradition treats it
neither as the only respectable regime nor as a self-stabilising one.
Plato and Aristotle catalogue it as one type among several; Polybius
makes the Roman version the centerpiece of his analysis; Cicero's{" "}
<cite>De Re Publica</cite> is the most sustained ancient defence of it
as a way of life.

## The Roman case and its weight

Most of what later European political thought meant by *republic* it
took from Rome. The mixed constitution Polybius analyses in Book VI —
consuls, senate, popular assemblies — was read as a working answer to
the problem the cycle of regimes (anakuklōsis) set out: each pure form
decays into its corrupt double, but a regime with balanced institutions
can resist that arc. Cicero gives the answer its most influential
formulation. The long shadow that formulation casts — through the
medieval communes, the Italian republics, the English commonwealth
writers, and the American founding — is the reason this theme is
central to the platform.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

that it depends on the character of its citizens — on civic virtue —
