## Nature does nothing in vain

Teleology — from *telos*, "end" or "purpose" — is the conviction that things
have *ends* toward which they are directed, and that to understand anything is
to grasp its purpose. The platform reads it as the framework underlying the
whole of Aristotle's philosophy: his biology, his physics, his ethics and his
politics. "Nature does nothing in vain," Aristotle holds; every natural thing
has a function it is for, a full development at which it aims, as the acorn aims
at the oak and the eye at seeing. To know a thing's nature is to know its end.

## The four causes and the final cause

The platform reads Aristotle's doctrine of the "four causes" as the structure
of his teleology. To explain anything fully, he argues, is to give its matter,
its form, its origin of motion, and — most importantly — its *final cause*, the
end *for the sake of which* it exists. The final cause is the one modern science
largely abandoned, holding that nature has no purposes; but the platform reads
Aristotle's teleology as retaining its force precisely in the domain of *living
things* and *human action*, where ends and functions remain indispensable to
understanding. A heart is *for* pumping blood; a human being is *for*
something too.

## Teleology in ethics and politics

The platform reads Aristotle's ethics and politics as thoroughly teleological.
The good for a thing is the full realization of its nature, its *telos*; so the
good for a human being is [eudaimonia](/themes/human-flourishing), the complete
exercise of our distinctive rational and social nature. And because human beings
are by nature *political animals*, whose nature is fulfilled only in the
community of the [polis](/themes/citizenship-in-aristotle), the city itself
exists *by nature* and *for the sake of* the good life — the famous claim that
"the polis exists by nature, and man is by nature a political animal." The
platform reads Aristotle's whole political philosophy as resting on this
teleological foundation: the city is judged by how well it serves its end, the
flourishing of its citizens.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

Teleology is the metaphysical foundation of Aristotle's practical philosophy and
the source of its distinctive character — the insistence that ethics and
politics are about the *fulfilment of human nature*, not the satisfaction of
arbitrary preference. The platform reads it as one of the most consequential
frameworks in the history of thought, shaping medieval philosophy and theology
profoundly, and as the ground of Aristotle's enduring claim that there is a
human good rooted in what we are, taken up in [why Aristotle
matters](/essays/why-aristotle-matters).
