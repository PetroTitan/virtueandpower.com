## The exemplum

The classical world taught conduct largely through *examples* — the
*exemplum*, the remembered figure or deed held up as a pattern to imitate
or avoid. The platform reads *historical examples* as the form of
instruction Plutarch raised to its highest pitch. A historical example is
not a rule but a *case*: a particular person in a particular situation,
acting well or badly, whose conduct the reader can weigh, remember and
carry. The Roman tradition was saturated with such examples, and Plutarch
gave the Greek and Roman past its richest single gallery of them.

## Why the case beats the rule

The platform reads the classical preference for the example over the rule
as a genuine claim about moral knowledge. Abstract rules tell you what to
do in general; examples show you what virtue and vice actually look like
in the texture of a life, where the hard cases live. Plutarch's wager is
that a reader learns more about courage from watching how a particular
man met a particular danger than from any definition of courage — and
more about the failure of integrity from watching Cato's rigidity in
action than from any maxim against inflexibility. The example carries the
particularity that the rule abstracts away, and particularity is where
conduct is actually decided.

## The portable pattern

What makes the historical example powerful is that it is *portable*. A
well-drawn example detaches from its original circumstances and becomes a
pattern the reader can apply to situations Plutarch never imagined. This
is how the Lives did their long work: a Renaissance prince, an English
parliamentarian, an American founder could read the example of a Roman or
a Greek and find in it a usable shape for his own conduct. The platform
reads this portability as the engine of Plutarch's influence — the reason
his figures kept being read as live counsel rather than dead history for
fifteen hundred years.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

This theme connects Plutarch to the platform's deepest editorial premise:
that the classical inheritance is worth reading because it still
instructs, and that it instructs best through concrete cases rather than
abstract doctrine. It underwrites the platform's whole approach of
reading figures and works for what they teach, and it is the formal basis
of [education through history](/themes/education-through-history) and of
the essay on [the uses of history](/essays/the-uses-of-history).
