## The first problem

Before a polity can pursue justice, virtue, prosperity or glory, it has
to secure the elementary good of order — a condition in which violence
is monopolised by the public power, conduct is predictable, contracts
hold, and the weak are not simply at the mercy of the strong. The
platform reads law and order as the *first* problem every founder must
solve, because everything else the political tradition values is built
on it. Hammurabi's prologue states the purpose plainly: the laws exist
"that the strong might not oppress the weak." The order is the
precondition; the justice is what the order is supposed to be *for*.

## Order as a good — and as an alibi

Law and order is a genuine good and also the most reliable alibi for
tyranny, and the platform holds both facts together. The promise of
order has justified some of the most durable and humane settlements in
history and some of the most brutal. The Legalist tradition in China
made order the supreme and almost the only political value:
[Han Fei](/philosophers/han-fei) argued that severe, certain,
impersonal punishment was the surest road to a peaceful realm, and the
Qin proved that the argument could build an empire and could not, by
itself, hold one. The platform reads the perennial tension here as the
distinction between order that serves the governed and order imposed
for its own sake — the line that separates
[law and order](/themes/law-and-order) from
[tyranny](/themes/tyranny).

## The disciplines of order

Different traditions secured order by different instruments, and the
platform reads them comparatively. Sparta secured it through
integrated civic-military [discipline](/themes/discipline-and-order);
Babylon and Rome through published law and courts; the Chinese
imperial state through a synthesis of Legalist administration and
Confucian moral formation. What they share is the recognition that
order is not natural — that it is built, maintained at cost, and
always at risk of dissolving back into the war of the strong against
the weak that the law exists to prevent.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

Law and order is the floor of the whole founders cluster — the good
that founders are first called to provide and in whose name much is
forgiven. The platform reads it without either the naïveté that treats
order as automatically just or the cynicism that treats every order as
mere domination. The essay on
[order versus charisma](/essays/order-versus-charisma) takes up the
question of whether durable order is better secured by impersonal
institutions or by the magnetism of a single ruler.
