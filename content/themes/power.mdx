## The classical inquiry

Power is the older question and a harder one to handle because the
word itself does not mean the same thing in every period. The Greek
*dunamis* and *kratos* and the Roman *imperium*, *potestas* and
*auctoritas* are not interchangeable, and the differences are
philosophically load-bearing.

Plato's <a href="/books/republic">Republic</a> opens with
Thrasymachus' definition of justice as the interest of the stronger
— that is, with power identified with rule and rule with the right
of the powerful. The rest of the dialogue is a sustained argument
against that identification: a defence of the proposition that power
without the right ordering of the soul of the one who holds it
collapses into something less than rule, both for the holder and
for those subject to it.

Aristotle's *Politics* keeps the careful distinction between rule
that is for the good of the ruled and rule that is for the benefit
of the ruler only. The medieval distinction between *potestas* and
*auctoritas* — power one has and the standing on which others
willingly defer to it — formalises a related point.

## What the tradition adds

The Roman literature on *imperium* and *auctoritas* (Cicero, the
historians, the legal tradition) shapes the Western political
vocabulary down to the modern period. Augustine's analysis of the
*libido dominandi* in the *City of God* presses the question of
what desire for power does to the person who has it. The realist
early-modern tradition (Machiavelli most explicitly) takes up the
question of how a ruler should act when the relation between
virtue and necessity is genuinely difficult.

The modern concept of *sovereignty* (Bodin, Hobbes and after) is a
distinctively early-modern construction; the classical vocabulary
maps onto it only partially. We treat the differences as
philosophically real rather than collapsing them.
