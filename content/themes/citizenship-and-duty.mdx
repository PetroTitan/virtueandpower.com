## Membership as a bond, not a status

Citizenship in the classical sense is not a passive legal status but a
*reciprocal bond*: the polity confers a share in its common life, and
the citizen owes the polity specific duties in return. Aristotle
defined the citizen, in the <cite>Politics</cite>, not by residence or
descent but by *participation* — the citizen is one who shares in
ruling and being ruled in turn. The platform reads citizenship-and-duty
as the human substance of the constitutional cluster: a constitution
specifies offices and procedures, but it is the citizen's sense of
owed obligation that makes those offices work.

## What the polity asked of its members

The ancient polities asked a great deal, and the platform reads the
range. Sparta asked everything: the citizen was a full-time soldier,
formed by the *agōgē* from the age of seven, his private life almost
wholly absorbed into the civic one. Athens after the reforms of Solon
and Cleisthenes asked active participation — service on juries, in the
assembly, in office by lot — and treated withdrawal into private life
with suspicion. The Confucian tradition built duty into a graded order
of relationships: the obligations of ruler and minister, parent and
child, were the threads from which a well-ordered society was woven.
Each tradition answered differently the question of *how much* the
polity may demand of a person.

## The duty of the founder's heirs

The founders-and-lawgivers cluster gives this theme a particular edge.
A constitution outlives its founder only if successive generations of
citizens accept its claims on them as binding — if they will serve,
pay, fight and obey not because they are compelled in each instance but
because they understand themselves to *owe* it. The platform reads the
decay of this sense of owed duty as one of the recurring ways orders
fail: the late Roman Republic kept its offices long after its citizens
had stopped treating the duties of citizenship as prior to private
advantage. The essay on
[the invention of citizenship](/essays/the-invention-of-citizenship)
traces where the idea came from.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

This theme binds the cluster's political concerns to the platform's
founding concern with [civic virtue](/themes/civic-virtue). Citizenship
is the institutional form; duty is its moral content; together they are
what a constitution needs from the people if it is to be more than
parchment. The platform reads the cultivation of that sense of duty —
through [education](/themes/education), ritual, and shared memory — as
one of the central tasks the great lawgivers set themselves.
