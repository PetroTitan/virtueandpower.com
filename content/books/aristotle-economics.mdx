## Historical context and authorship

The Economics (*Oeconomica*) is the treatise on household and estate management
transmitted under Aristotle's name, and the platform reads it with an honest
note about its authorship: only the first of its books is plausibly close to
Aristotle himself, while the others are generally regarded as the work of his
Peripatetic followers. The platform treats it, accordingly, as a work of the
*Aristotelian school* rather than securely of the master — but as a genuine and
valuable link in the classical tradition of writing on the *oikos*, the
household, which the Greeks regarded as the basic economic and social unit.

## Central argument

The platform reads the Economics as developing the account of the household
Aristotle gives at the opening of the [Politics](/books/politics). For
Aristotle, the household (*oikos*) is the foundation of the city: the city is a
community of households, and the art of managing a household — its property, its
members, its production — is the foundation of the art of governing a city. The
treatise treats the relationships of the household (husband and wife, parent and
child, master and servant), the acquisition and management of property, and the
ordering of the estate. The platform reads it under [governance through
character](/themes/governance-through-character): the management of the household
is a school of the practical virtues that scale up to the management of the
state.

## Significance

The platform reads the Economics as significant for the continuity it shows
between the classical reflection on the household and the later European
tradition of "economics" — a word that descends, like the discipline's earliest
form, from this Greek concern with the *oikos*. It stands in the line that runs
from Xenophon's [Oeconomicus](/books/oeconomicus), through this Aristotelian
treatise, to the Roman agricultural writers and the long tradition of estate
management. The platform reads it as evidence that the ancients understood the
economy first as a matter of the well-ordered household, and only by extension
of the city and the empire.

## Reception and influence

The platform reads the Economics as a minor but instructive work in the
Aristotelian corpus — read for centuries as Aristotle's, and influential on the
medieval and early-modern tradition of household and estate management. The
platform carries it with its authorship clearly flagged, as the Aristotelian
contribution to the classical literature on the household economy, and reads it
alongside Xenophon's [Oeconomicus](/books/oeconomicus) as the two foundational
Greek treatments of the subject.
