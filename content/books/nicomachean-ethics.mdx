## What it is

The *Nicomachean Ethics* is one of three ethical treatises associated
with Aristotle (the others are the *Eudemian Ethics* and the *Magna
Moralia*). Its conventional title comes from its association with
Aristotle's son, Nicomachus. The work survives in ten books and
belongs, on the standard view, to the period of the Lyceum.

It is the foundational treatise of what is now called *virtue
ethics*: an inquiry into the good life and the kind of person who
lives it, rather than into rules of action or rights and duties
abstracted from character.

## What it argues

The treatise opens by asking what the human good is, and answers in
terms of *eudaimonia* — usually translated "happiness," more precisely
something like "flourishing," "doing-and-being-well." It locates that
flourishing in activity in accordance with virtue (*aretē*) over the
course of a complete life.

The middle books develop the famous doctrine that virtues of
character lie in a mean between two failings of excess and
deficiency — courage between rashness and cowardice, generosity
between prodigality and stinginess — and that this mean is determined
by *phronēsis*, the practical wisdom of the person of good character
in a particular situation. Book V is the classical treatment of
justice (distributive and corrective); Book VI distinguishes the
intellectual virtues; Books VIII–IX are the long, careful treatment
of friendship that has shaped the subject in Western philosophy ever
since; Book X considers pleasure and closes with the question of the
contemplative life.

## Why it matters for Virtue & Power

The *Ethics* is the platform's central reference for the meaning of
virtue, the role of practical wisdom in leadership and rule, and the
relation between character and action. The vocabulary it sets — virtue,
mean, prudence, friendship, flourishing — is the vocabulary the
classical and medieval traditions used to think about how to live.
