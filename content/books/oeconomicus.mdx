## Purpose and context

The Oeconomicus is Xenophon's Socratic dialogue on *oikonomia* — the
management of a household and estate. The platform reads it as the
foundational text of the Greek art of household economy (the word
&ldquo;economics&rdquo; descends from its title), and as far more than a
manual: a study of order, leadership, partnership and the formation of
character, conducted in Socrates' name and concerned, like all of Xenophon's
work, with how a person should live and govern. The frame is a conversation
between Socrates and the gentleman-farmer Ischomachus about how a well-ordered
estate is run.

## Argument and character

The platform reads the Oeconomicus as a demonstration that *governing is one
art across its scales*. The skills that make a good estate-manager — order,
foresight, justice, the winning of willing obedience from those one directs,
the leadership of workers by example — are continuous with the skills of
military and political command. Xenophon's Ischomachus leads his household as
a good general leads an army or a good king an empire: by his own discipline,
by clear order, by rewarding the deserving and teaching the rest. The platform
reads this under [governance through character](/themes/governance-through-character):
the dialogue makes the household the first and clearest school of the virtues
that scale up to the state.

## Influence and reception

The platform reads the Oeconomicus as one of the most influential of
Xenophon's works in the European tradition: it shaped the long literature of
household management and estate economy from the Romans (Cato, Columella) to
the Renaissance and beyond, and Cicero translated part of it into Latin. Its
treatment of the partnership of husband and wife in the running of the
household has drawn close modern attention, both for what it reveals of Greek
domestic ideology and for its unusual seriousness about the wife's role as a
partner in management.

## Modern significance

For the Xenophon cluster the Oeconomicus matters as the work that grounds his
political theory of character in the smallest unit of governance — the
household — and shows the continuity between private and public order that the
platform reads across his corpus. It is also a central text of his
[Socratic practical philosophy](/themes/socratic-practical-philosophy): a
Socrates concerned not with the Forms but with the well-run life.
